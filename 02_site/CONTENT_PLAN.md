# Content Plan

**Purpose:** a light framework for thinking about what content formats to use and at what cadence. Not a publishing calendar. Not a commitment. A guide.

---

## Core principle

**Don't commit to a cadence. Commit to a quality bar.**

The fastest way to kill a personal site is to promise "weekly essays" or "two videos a month" and then fall behind. The second-fastest way is to publish mediocre content to keep the schedule. Avoid both.

Publish when a piece is ready. The reader would rather wait two months for something good than read something rushed every week.

---

## Format mix — three content types

Each has a different strength, cost, and purpose.

### 1. Written essays
**What it's for:** deep thinking. Anything where a reader benefits from pausing, re-reading, sitting with an idea. Trading psychology, market observations, reflections on specific trading experiences, analytical breakdowns, opinion pieces.

**Cost to produce:** 4-10 hours per piece for a well-edited essay. The cheapest format per piece.

**Strengths:**
- Searchable and referenceable long after publication
- Compound over time (an essay from 2026 still has value in 2030)
- Can be polished through multiple drafts
- Low friction to start, low friction to publish

**Weaknesses:**
- Smaller audience reach in 2026 than video
- Harder to "spread" virally

**Realistic cadence:** varies. Could be 1-2 essays per month if writing-active, could be one every 3 months if not. No schedule.

### 2. Video
**What it's for:** topics that benefit from visualization, narrative structure, or emotional weight. Deep dives where motion helps the explanation. Pieces where a voice carries the thinking better than text.

**Cost to produce:** varies wildly by format.
- Talking-head with cutaways: 3-8 hours per finished minute
- Motion graphics explainer: 2-5 hours per finished minute
- Full animation (Kurzgesagt-style): 10-20+ hours per finished minute

For a solo creator in 2026, **motion graphics + voiceover is the realistic sustainable format.** Tools like Motion, After Effects, or even Figma + screen capture can produce beautiful work without requiring a studio.

**Strengths:**
- Dramatically larger potential audience than writing
- Emotional and narrative power
- Showcases design and craft in a way text can't
- More shareable

**Weaknesses:**
- High production cost per piece
- Slow iteration cycle
- Harder to revise after publication

**Realistic cadence:** one good video per 4-8 weeks if video is an active focus. One every few months if not.

### 3. Experiments
**What it's for:** small standalone tools, calculators, interactive pieces, visualizations. Anything where "build the thing" is faster or better than "write about the thing."

**Cost to produce:** varies.
- A simple calculator or visualization: 4-20 hours
- A mini-tool with some interactivity: 20-60 hours
- A full product experiment (like the Playbook tool if ever built): hundreds of hours

**Strengths:**
- Genuinely useful for readers who use them
- Differentiates the site from pure content
- Can compound (an experiment made once is used forever)
- Fun to make

**Weaknesses:**
- No audience reach unless promoted or linked from content
- Maintenance burden if they break
- Can become distractions from writing/video

**Realistic cadence:** ad-hoc. Build when the itch strikes. Don't force one.

## What to write/make about

**The spine of the site:** the psychology of trading, especially in the prop firm space. Human error, fear, greed, self-deception, the split between who you think you are as a trader and who you actually are. The subject matter is rich and most of the existing content in the space is shallow. Room to go deep.

**Adjacent topics that legitimately fit:**
- Specific trading concepts done with care (e.g. "what happens in your head in the first 5 minutes after a loss")
- Prop firm analysis (comparative, critical, not promotional)
- Reflections on specific days, specific trades, specific setups
- Observations about the futures market structure, instrument behavior, session dynamics
- Reviews or critiques of other tools/platforms/content in the space
- Interview-style pieces with other traders (eventually, when the site has gravity)

**What to avoid:**
- Signals, trade calls, "what I'm trading today" content
- Performance flexing / P&L screenshot culture
- Prop firm affiliate content
- Generic finance content that isn't trader-specific
- AI-generated filler
- Listicles ("5 ways to improve your trading")

The instinct test: would a serious trader read this and think "finally, someone who gets it"? If yes, make it. If it reads like thousands of other trading blog posts, don't.

## First content at launch

v0 of the site needs something on it when it goes live. Realistic options:

**Option A:** one strong essay that sets the tone. Could be about psychology, specific trading experience, or a manifesto-style piece. The MANIFESTO already in `01_brand/brand_guidelines/` could be adapted as an essay, or a completely new piece.

**Option B:** one strong essay plus an "about" page that's substantive — treating the about as a content piece in itself, not just a bio.

**Option C:** two shorter essays that together establish what the site is about.

**Recommendation:** Option A or B. One great piece is better than two mediocre ones. If you have a draft sitting somewhere (on paper, in Notion, in your head), that's the first piece.

---

## After launch — what to watch

Once the site is live, pay attention to two signals:

1. **What do you want to make?** Which piece is pulling at you? Write/make that.
2. **What do readers respond to?** If an essay gets unusual engagement, make more in that vein. If nothing is resonating, the site is still young — don't overreact.

Don't do:
- Keyword research to chase SEO
- "Topic planning" in spreadsheets
- Committing to "series" you won't finish
- Writing for engagement rather than for yourself

The best personal sites come from people writing what they genuinely want to write about. Sincerity is legible, fakeness is too.

If/when the Playbook tool (in `04_future_experiments/playbook/`) gets revisited, it can enter the site as an experiment — either a standalone tool at `/experiments/playbook` or (if it grows) as a separate product with its own subdomain that the main site links to.

Until then, it stays parked.
